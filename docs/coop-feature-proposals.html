The single rule that makes co-op feel good

The It Takes Two north star

The reason that game feels magical isn't the set pieces — it's that designers gave each player diverse but complementary abilities, so neither person can solve the puzzle alone. Combat becomes a conversation. We want every idea below to pass one test:

Test #1

Each player holds half

If one strong player could just carry the other, it's not co-op — it's a babysitter sim. The best ideas split the solution itself: you have a piece I don't, and vice-versa.

Test #2

Talking is the gameplay

You and your gf are sitting together. Force the table-talk: "It's the one on the left!" "No, the feminine one!" The mechanic should make your mouths move, not just your thumbs.

Test #3

Shared fate, shared brag

One scoreboard. One streak. One boss. When it lands, you both did it. When it breaks, you both feel it — and you immediately want to run it back.

🧭
Where this fits todayYour app already has the seed: PvE mode (shared hint pool, "two players sitting together") and the Memory Game prototype (P1 only flips English, P2 only flips Spanish). These ideas are the natural next rooms in that house — not a new house.
⚠️
The anti-pattern to avoid"Two people doing the same thing at the same time." That's just solo ×2 with extra latency. If an idea works fine when one person plays both roles, it failed Test #1 — push it harder or drop it.
Five rules I held every idea to

Design pillars

The north star is one big idea; these are the five working principles I used to keep the good and cut the filler. If an idea broke a pillar, it got reworked or dropped. They're opinionated on purpose — argue with them.

🧩

1 · Two halves, one answer

Split the solution, not just the workload. The answer should live between you — impossible to produce from one screen alone.

✗ Fails if one person can solo both roles.
🗣️

2 · Make their mouths move

The phone is a prop; the conversation is the gameplay. Every mechanic should give you a reason to talk across the couch.

✗ Fails if you can play in total silence.
❤️

3 · One heartbeat

Shared score, shared streak, shared boss. You rise and fall as a unit. The brag is always "we did it," never "I won."

✗ Fails if it's just two solo scores side by side.
⚖️

4 · Carry, don't crush

A real couple has a skill gap. The stronger player should be able to help — never to carry-and-bench the other into a passenger.

✗ Fails if the weaker player can zone out.
🍮

5 · Warm, not ruthless

Your voice is cheeky-loving, not toxic. Co-op should feel like a hug with stakes — tense, silly, and kind even when you lose.

✗ Fails if losing makes someone actually grumpy.
📐
How to use theseWhen you're deciding what to build, score a candidate against all five. The flagships (#02 Relay, #04 Decoder, #06 Call & Response) hit 5/5. The cozy ones (#10 Bridge) trade pillar 3's intensity for calm — which is a deliberate choice, not an accident.

01
Seed · the mock memory game

Two-Minds Memory

Classic memory match, but the deck is split by language — and the board only moves when you take turns.

DEPENDENCY: complementary halves + shared memory REUSES MemoryGame.tsx CO-LOCATED ✨
How it works
  1. ENP1 can only flip English cards. ESP2 can only flip Spanish cards. The other person's cards are physically un-tappable for you.
  2. A "round" is one EN flip + one ES flip. So every single match requires both of you to act — there is no solo move.
  3. You're each holding half the board's memory. P1 remembers where "madre" probably was; P2 remembers where "mother" went. You have to say it out loud.
  4. Match → the player who didn't start the last round starts the next one, so turn pressure keeps swapping. Clear all pairs to win together.
🧠
Why it's funMemory is already social agony ("I KNOW we saw it!"). Splitting the deck turns your two imperfect memories into one shared brain — neither of you can brute-force it alone.
🗣️
Forces table-talk"I flipped mother — where's madre?!" is the entire game. This is the purest version of your co-located thesis.

Design detail · scoring, edge cases, balancing, build lane

Scoring & economy

No PvP score — the only number is rounds to clear (lower = better) and your shared best. Optional: consecutive matches feed a streak glow (modifier #08); a perfect "no mismatch" board earns a 🌟.

Edge cases

  • During the 0.9s mismatch reveal, both boards lock (no double-flip).
  • Tapping the partner's board does nothing — enforced, not just dimmed.
  • Odd theme sizes: pad to an even pair count or drop the orphan.

Mixed-skill (pillar 4)

Give the beginner the language they're learning toward (Spanish) for max exposure, or run Blind mode as the expert's handicap — the stronger player can't see their own board and must rely on the partner's narration.

02
Seed · build sentences one word at a time

Sentence Relay

Build one Spanish sentence together — but you alternate, placing exactly one word per turn, and you each only hold half the word bank.

DEPENDENCY: split word bank + turn lock REUSES RebuildSentenceBeta.tsx CO-LOCATED ✨
How it works
  1. One target sentence, e.g. "I write at night" → Yo · escribo · por · la · noche. The scrambled tokens are dealt between you: P1 holds half, P2 holds the other half.
  2. Turns alternate. On your turn you place exactly one token from your hand into the shared sentence tray, then it's the other person's turn.
  3. Because the correct next word might be in your partner's hand, you constantly have to hand off — "okay I need a verb next, you got escribo?"
  4. Build the full sentence in the right order, then either of you hits Check. Wrong order = it shakes; talk it out and rebuild.
🧩
Why it's funIt's a tiny improv scene. Neither hand can finish the sentence, so you're forced to plan word order together and predict what the other holds.
🪢
The interdependenceSplit bank + strict alternation = you can stall the whole sentence by being out of sync. Real co-op friction, in a good way.

Knobs & variants

  • No-undo hard mode — placed words lock. One bad turn means you finish wrong and laugh about it.

⚠️
Watch outIf both hands can place the same needed word, the dependency softens. Deal so that order-critical words (subject, verb) sit in opposite hands.
Design detail · scoring, edge cases, balancing, build lane

Scoring & economy

Track hand-offs and rebuilds; a zero-undo sentence is a 🌟. Chain a shared streak across sentences for a session score — gives the cozy mode some spine.

Edge cases · deadlocks

  • Deal owners alternating along the correct order → strict alternation is always solvable (that's exactly what the playable demo does — try to break it).
  • Wrong order = shake + Undo, never a hard fail.
  • No-undo hard mode: lock placed tokens for chaos.

Mixed-skill (pillar 4)

Beginner holds the function words (por, la, en) and the shorter hand; expert holds verbs/subjects. Or run Telephone: beginner sees the English meaning & directs, expert holds tokens but is "blind" to the goal.

03
Seed · throw words to your partner for help

Word Toss

Split a challenge's words between you, race a shared clock — and fling the ones you're stuck on across to your partner to answer.

DEPENDENCY: divided workload + rescue economy REUSES duel question/answer engine NEW: toss + shared timer
How it works
  1. A challenge of, say, 20 words is dealt 10 to each of you. One shared countdown for the whole team.
  2. You blast through your own stack. Hit one you blank on? Toss it — it flies out of your queue and lands in your partner's "Incoming" tray.
  3. Tossed words are worth a little less (or cost a shared "toss token") so you can't just dump your whole stack — you triage together.
  4. Team wins when every word across both stacks is answered before the clock hits zero. One timer, one result.
🤝
Why it's funIt models real studying together: "ugh I always forget this one — here, you take it." The toss is a tiny act of trust, and catching a save feels great.
⏱️
The interdependenceA shared clock + divided list means you're balancing two workloads. A strong player can help but the toss budget stops them from just doing everything.

Design detail · economy, edge cases, balancing, build lane

The toss economy

  • Each toss costs a shared token (or a few points) → triage, not dumping.
  • Catch bonus: answering a rescue fast nudges the team streak — catching feels heroic.
  • Boomerang once: P2 can lob it back; a second miss banks it for "learn together" after.
  • Incoming-tray cap stops one player drowning the other.

Edge cases

Clock hits zero with words left = team loss, but reveal every answer so it's a study moment, not a wall.

Mixed-skill (pillar 4)

Deal the learner fewer / easier words and cheaper tosses; the expert's catch bonus pays them for helping, not for hoarding a giant stack.

Build lane

LANE B · live The one mode here that's natively two-device: reuses the duel answer engine + per-player queues + a shared timer on the doc. An async variant works too — tosses arrive as notifications.

04
Asymmetric info · guide & follower

Decoder Duo

One of you can see the word. The other is the only one who can answer it. Neither screen is enough — your voices bridge the gap.

DEPENDENCY: split information (guide ↔ follower) REUSES duel options engine + TTS CO-LOCATED ✨✨
How it works
  1. ENP1 = the Clue. Sees the Spanish word + a little hint. Has no answer buttons — can look but can't tap.
  2. ESP2 = the Pick. Sees four English options and is the only one who can tap. But P2 can't see the Spanish word at all.
  3. P1 has to talk P2 onto the answer — describe it, act it out, give a Spanish synonym (your call how strict). P2 decides and taps.
  4. Roles swap every round, so you both practise recall and describing. Right answer = shared point.
🕵️
Why it's funIt's Keep Talking and Nobody Explodes for vocab. The person who knows can't click; the person who clicks doesn't know. Pure dependency, tons of laughing.
🔬
Grounded in researchThis is the classic co-op "guide & follower" split — proven to force collaborative dialogue and corrective feedback. (See sources.)

Design detail · the "legal clue" rule, balancing, build lane

House-rule difficulty dial

  • Casual: describe freely in English, just don't say the answer word.
  • Spicy: no English root of the answer, no rhymes.
  • Fluent: 🔥 Spanish-only clues. This is the real workout.

Scoring

Team correct / total; roles swap every card so describing & recall both get reps. Optional shared timer per word to stop over-describing.

Mixed-skill (pillar 4)

Put the stronger speaker on describe (productive, hard) and the learner on pick (recognition, easier) — then flip when they're ready. The handicap is literally which role you take.

Build lane & the killer reuse

LANE A · hot-seat trivial (playable above). For two-device, canRevealQuestionAnswer in getDuel already hides answers per viewer — render the prompt to the clue-giver and the options to the picker; the hidden-info plumbing exists.

Literally half each

Split-Letter Forge

Spell the Spanish answer by dragging letters into a shared tray — except the letters are split across both your phones.

DEPENDENCY: split letter pool → shared tray REUSES Anagram hint logic NEW: shared drag tray CO-LOCATED ✨
How it works
  1. Prompt: English meaning → spell the Spanish. The answer's scrambled letters are dealt across two phones — P1 gets some tiles, P2 gets the rest.
  2. There's one shared tray of empty slots. Either of you can drop a letter into the next slot, but you only own your tiles.
  3. Since the next letter you need might be in your partner's hand, you call it out: "I need an 'i' for slot 5 — that's you."
  4. Fill every slot in order to forge the word. The tray glows green when it matches.
⚒️
Why it's funIt's the most literal "each holds half." You can see the missing letter sitting in your partner's hand and can't grab it — you have to ask.
✍️
BonusSpelling/production is harder than recognition, so this trains a deeper skill than multiple-choice — together.

06
Ears vs eyes · leans on your TTS

Call & Response

One of you only hears the word. The other only sees the spellings. Say what you heard; pick what you saw.

DEPENDENCY: audio ↔ text split REUSES useTTS + theme audio CO-LOCATED ✨
How it works
  1. ENP1 = Ears. Taps play and hears the Spanish word via TTS. No text on screen. Must repeat it out loud as best they can.
  2. ESP2 = Eyes. Sees four very similar Spanish spellings (e.g. cocina / cocinar / cocido / cosina) but hears nothing. Picks the one P1 is saying.
  3. This nails the hardest real-world skill: hearing Spanish and mapping it to spelling — and it makes P1 actually pronounce things ("oral mastery", per your thesis).
  4. Swap ears/eyes each round. Correct = shared point + the word's meaning revealed to both.
🎧
Why it's funP1 mangling a rolled "r" while P2 squints at near-identical spellings is peak couch comedy — and sneakily great listening + pronunciation practice.
🗣️
On-thesisYour tagline is literally about oral mastery. This is the only mode where someone must speak for the team to score.

Design detail · distractors, balancing, build lane

Making it hard the right way

The options should be minimal pairs — words differing by one sound the ear must catch: b/v (bello/vello), c/s (cocer/coser), ll/y, single vs double vowels. Auto-build distractor sets from these so the listening is real, not guessable.

Scoring

Pillar 4, made concrete

The mixed-skill problem

This is the one that quietly kills couple co-op. You two won't have identical Spanish. If the stronger player can carry, the other becomes a passenger; if the weaker player is a pure bottleneck, they feel bad. Both ruin the night. Here's the toolbox for tuning every mode so the gap becomes texture, not friction.

🪨
Failure A · The carryStrong player does everything; weak player taps "next" and disengages. Looks cooperative, feels like babysitting.
🧱
Failure B · The bottleneckEvery stall is the weak player's fault, in the open, on a shared clock. Shame is not a study aid.
Six levers to balance any mode
Lever 1

Asymmetric difficulty

Same round, different hardness per player. Beginner gets 3 options; expert gets 4 + "none of the above" or must type. Your engine already varies difficulty by question index — make it per-player.

Reuses lib/difficultyUtils + answerShuffle wrongCount.

Lever 2

Direction split

Beginner answers ES→EN (recognition, easier). Expert answers EN→ES (production, harder). Decoder & Co-op Boss already lean on direction — bake it into the handicap.

Lever 3

Handicap clocks

The trailing learner simply gets more seconds per card. On shared-clock modes, weight their correct answers a touch higher so effort, not raw speed, wins.

Lever 4

Role by strength

Hand the demanding role to whoever's stronger: the describer in Decoder, the shield-breaker in Boss, the bigger hand in Relay. The weaker player still does the decisive act.

Lever 5

Rubber-banding

When the gap widens mid-game, quietly feed the trailing player easier or higher-value cards to pull them back into contention. Catch-up, not charity.

Lever 6

The mentor loop

Best of all: design so the strong player's optimal move is to teach — gift a hint (#14), take the describe role, set up the easy pass — never to hoard the turns.

The one genuinely new primitive: turns

Today, duels have no "whose turn" — both players answer the same question in parallel and the round advances when both are done. Turn-based co-op (Relay, Memory, Bridge, Boss windows) needs an explicit activePlayer on the doc plus a guard — mirror your existing assertDuelMode with an assertActivePlayer. That's the main net-new backend concept; almost everything else is fields + a mutation.

The objections I'd raise if I were you

Honest FAQ

Pre-empting the doubts, because a proposal that only sells itself is useless.

"We're not always in the same room."

Fair — co-located is the best case, not the only one. The Same room? column in the matrix flags it. Word Toss, One Streak, Lifeline, and Co-op Boss all work across two devices/async; the hidden-info ones (Decoder, Call & Response) are where being together really sings. Filter the matrix by "Same-room best" to separate them.

"Do we really need two phones?"

No. Every flagship has a single-device hot-seat version — that's exactly what the playground demos are. Pass the phone for Decoder/Memory/Relay/Password. Two devices only matter when you both need to see different secret info at the same time.

"Isn't this just trivia with extra steps?"

That's the failure mode pillar 1 guards against. The test: if a mode plays fine with one person doing both roles, it is just trivia and I'd cut it. The keepers force a hand-off, a description, or a shared risk — the "extra step" is the togetherness.

"Our Spanish levels are really different."

Good — that's a feature here, not a bug. The whole mixed-skill section is about turning the gap into roles: the stronger player describes/teaches, the learner picks/decides. Start with the free social fix (play your weaker direction) before touching code.

"Won't this fragment the app?"

Co-op slots under one umbrella (e.g. Juntos) beside Solo and Duel. Every mode reuses the same Themes + answer engine, and the backend changes are optional & mode-gated, so Solo/PvP/PvE are untouched. It's additive, not a rewrite.

"What stops cheating in Decoder?"

Three answers: it's you and your gf (who's cheating whom?); house rules set the clue difficulty; and for two-device play, your existing canRevealQuestionAnswer genuinely hides the word from the picker's client. Cheat-proof if you want it.
